#LeadingTheWay
Advancing Women in the Mortgage Industry

Jodi Morton

Vice President, Chief Data Officer
Single-Family Division

"I have been in this business for 25 years and was often was the only female in the room," says Jodi Morton, vice president and chief data officer in Freddie Mac's Single-Family division. "I didn't think I fit in at all. Who was my peer group? My peer group were mostly men whose wives stayed home and raised their children."

You can't look at Ms. Morton's career without noting that she's a woman who's risen to the top in a largely male-dominated industry. Several years ago, when she took on the chief data officer role, she wasn't just one of the only women in the room; she was also one of the only people without an information technology background. Now when she goes to data conferences, she says she's one of several women chief data officers and she's pleased to see that many of them have broad business backgrounds.

In an environment of ever-changing and evolving business needs and technology, Ms. Morton is responsible for the development and implementation of the Single-Family data strategy, which includes:

  • Data governance, policies, standards and practices.
  • Data quality transparency and reporting.
  • Advancement of data acquisition strategies and analytic capabilities.

In 2014, she received a crucially important request from Single-Family's Executive Vice President Dave Lowman: "Go fix our data problem." Since then and under Ms. Morton's leadership, Freddie Mac has morphed into a company that's harnessing the power of data and advanced analytics like never before. 

Ms. Morton also leads by example as a member of Freddie Mac's employee resource groups, including Working Parents and Women's Interactive Network (WIN), where her projects such as teaching "Coaching Your Inner Critic," have been well received.  In 2017, she was chosen as Freddie Mac's Working Mother of the Year. And you don't have to sift through a mountain of data to see why HousingWire recently named her a 2018 Woman of Influence.

She believes the most important issue women face in the workplace today is their own internal struggle and, despite her accolades, isn't immune to occasional self-doubt herself.

"Some of us want to be perfect on all fronts – perfect wife, mom, career woman," she says. "We need to fight our own inner critic. I am my own worst critic nine days out of 10. Sometimes you must take a step back, get some perspective and give yourself a break."

While Ms. Morton believes her male bosses have had the best intentions, she says they didn't always understand her issues as a working mother, and didn't know how to help her manage the competing demands.  

"The help I needed wasn't necessarily about improving my golf game or addressing a work issue," she says. "Often, it was how to keep my work and life in balance. Do men go through this same struggle in the same way? Probably not and I understand men have their own challenges. But I think women in particular struggle with being perfect across all of the roles we play."

She says she's passionate about supporting women in their quests to juggle their work and home lives since she's been there.

"I've come to believe there's no such thing as general work-life balance," she says. "It's unique for each person. We each must define what it is for ourselves. I have an unbelievably supportive husband who supported my career while he built his career. We split the home duties. He runs with his areas and I run with my mine. But I talk to a lot of women who don't have that support at home."

Ms. Morton can also relate to the challenges women starting out in their careers face since she recalls her own. "I spent a lot of time wondering how do I walk into a room? and how do I get heard in those rooms?"

"I remember several times when I spoke up but wasn't heard.  Then someone else shared the same idea and the team loved it," she says. "You don't want to be pushy but you don't want to be a pushover, either.  It's a tricky balance for women."

Based on her experience, two lessons she's learned and now shares with women trying to rise to leadership positions are:

  • Make sure you are always on your game. Do your homework and be prepared to demonstrate expertise and leadership.

  • Determine the most effective way to make your voice heard.  This takes practice and self-awareness of how you are perceived by others.

While she's proud to no longer be the only woman in the room, she wants organizations to continue their progress by focusing on these three areas:

  • Greater awareness building. Advancing more women in the workplace will require the understanding and support of both women and men. "We need to do a better job of engaging male leaders to help boost equality in the workplace," she says.

  • More flexible work arrangements. "They make a huge difference and radically change the way my day works," she says. "Let's improve the technology to enable flexibility and put our focus on achieving bottom line results."

  • Ongoing learning that's part of your job. "Ongoing learning should be an accepted part of every organization's culture," she says. "And you shouldn't have to take eight hours of training and then work eight extra hours to make up for it."
